The landing page math nobody teaches

D
By Doddy
Mar 28, 2026 • 7 min read

You can find a thousand posts listing "the 10 essential sections of a landing page." That's not the problem. The problem is everyone knows the sections and still builds pages that convert at 0.3%.

Here's the math they skip.

The 3-second rule is actually a 3-word rule

Visitors decide to stay or leave based on the first three words of your hero. Not three seconds. Three words.

Test: cover the rest of your hero with your hand. Read just the first three words. Do they tell a stranger what the product does and why they'd want it? If not, your page starts with zero context and loses on every scroll from there.

"Smell like you mean it" works. "Introducing our new range" doesn't. One has a verb and a reason. The other is a press release.

Word count per section

I measured my own site against 40 D2C landing pages that convert well. The ranges are tighter than you'd think:

  • Hero headline: 4–8 words
  • Hero subhead: 12–20 words
  • CTA button: 2–4 words, always a verb
  • Section headings: 3–6 words
  • Body copy per section: under 60 words

Go over these and scroll depth drops hard. The internet doesn't read — it skims.

The order nobody talks about

Every template puts sections in this order: hero → features → testimonials → pricing → FAQ. It's wrong for most D2C.

Better order for a physical product under ₹500:

  1. Hero — one sentence of what + one photo of it
  2. Pricing — yes, right here, second. Filter out tire-kickers early.
  3. Problem — one line that names the exact pain
  4. Solution — three bullets max
  5. Social proof — even if it's one screenshot
  6. Comparison table — vs. the category default
  7. FAQ — kills objections before checkout
  8. Final CTA — same button as hero, exact same text
→ Why pricing so early

Hiding price increases bounce rate, not conversion. People who bounce on price were never going to buy. People who stay past pricing are qualified, and every pixel after converts harder.

Button color — the real answer

Forget A/B testing orange vs. green. The rule is: your CTA should be the highest-contrast element on the page. If your brand is matte black, a bright lime button outconverts every "safe" dark button. If your brand is white/minimal, a saturated dark button wins.

Contrast > color theory.

The hierarchy test

Squint at your landing page from three feet away. What's the first thing you see? The second? The third?

It should be: headline → CTA → price. In that order. If the first thing you see is your logo or navigation, the page is upside-down.

Fix this one thing and conversion moves more than any headline test.
