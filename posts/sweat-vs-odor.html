Sweat Doesn't Stink. Bacteria Do. The Difference Matters.

Fresh sweat is almost odorless. The smell you're actually dealing with is bacterial waste. That's a different problem — and it has a different fix.

By Doddy Editorial ● April 22, 2026 ● 7 min read
Sweat ≠
smell.
Bacteria = smell.
TL;DR
  • Fresh sweat from most of your body is 99% water and salt — nearly odorless on its own.
  • The smell comes from skin bacteria converting apocrine secretions into sulfur compounds. It's bacterial waste, not sweat.
  • This means the solution is targeting bacteria on skin and fabric — not just blocking sweat.

Most hygiene marketing is built on one core confusion. It bundles sweat and smell into the same problem and sells you a product to solve both at once. Which would be great, except they're not the same problem. They have different chemistry, different timelines, and different solutions.

Understanding the difference won't just help you smell better. It'll save you money on products that are solving the wrong thing.

Two types of sweat glands, two completely different jobs

Your body has two main types of sweat glands and they do very different things.

Eccrine glands cover most of your body surface. They produce a watery, salty fluid — primarily water, sodium chloride, and a small amount of other electrolytes. This is cooling sweat. It evaporates and drops your core temperature. When it's fresh, it's essentially odorless. The faint "clean sweat" smell some people notice is from trace compounds, but nothing that registers as unpleasant to most people.

Apocrine glands are concentrated in your armpits, groin, and scalp. They don't start functioning until puberty. They produce a thicker fluid that contains proteins, lipids, and steroids — nutrients, essentially, for the bacteria that live on your skin.

Apocrine sweat is also, on its own, nearly odorless. The critical word is "on its own."

What bacteria actually do to your sweat

Your skin hosts a dense community of bacteria. In your armpits specifically, the dominant species include Staphylococcus hominis and various Corynebacterium strains. These bacteria feed on the proteins and lipids in apocrine sweat.

Researchers at the University of York pinpointed the exact mechanism in 2020. S. hominis contains a specific enzyme — C-T lyase — that cleaves an odorless precursor compound in apocrine sweat (Cys-Gly-3M3SH) and converts it into a thioalcohol. Thioalcohols are sulfur-containing molecules. Your nose is exquisitely sensitive to sulfur compounds. This is the primary molecule behind the characteristic armpit smell.

Fresh sweat is food. The bacteria eat it, and the waste is what smells.

The same process happens on your clothes. Once bacteria from your skin transfer onto fabric, they continue metabolizing sweat residue in the fibers. The smell isn't the sweat drying — it's the ongoing bacterial activity in the fabric, which is why shirts can smell worse the next day than they did right after you wore them.

Why this distinction matters for what you buy

Deodorant and antiperspirant solve different parts of this chain.

Deodorant targets the bacteria on your skin — using antimicrobial agents like triclosan or alcohol to reduce the bacterial population that converts sweat into smell. It doesn't stop sweat. It reduces the bacterial count so there's less conversion happening.

Antiperspirant targets the eccrine glands — using aluminum compounds to physically block the ducts and reduce sweat output. It doesn't kill bacteria. It reduces the volume of sweat available for bacteria to metabolize.

Both work on skin. Neither works on fabric. Once the bacteria have moved from your skin into your shirt — which happens every time you wear it — neither product reaches them.

The fabric problem nobody tells you about

Synthetic fabrics — polyester, nylon, elastane — are hydrophobic. They repel water. This sounds useful for a sports shirt, but it means bacteria-rich sweat isn't fully rinsed out during washing. The bacteria embed deeper in the fiber structure and survive a normal cold wash cycle intact.

Cotton is hydrophilic — it absorbs water — so bacteria tend to rinse out more completely. This is why cotton shirts often smell cleaner after washing even when they felt wetter during wear.

What the complete solution looks like

If you want to address body odor properly, you need to cover all three links in the chain:

Skin: Deodorant or antiperspirant reduces bacterial activity and sweat volume at the source. Use what works for your body — there's no universally superior option.

Fabric: A fabric-specific odor mist kills bacteria embedded in your clothes between wears and before you put them on. This is the gap neither deodorant nor antiperspirant covers. We wrote a full breakdown of which product does what if you want the comparison.

Washing: Hot water (60°C minimum) and smaller loads are needed to actually reduce bacterial load in synthetic fabrics. Cold water on its own doesn't kill the bacteria — it just moves them around. See our full guide on why clothes still smell after washing for the protocol.

The smell isn't you. It's the bacteria. That framing makes the solution obvious.

Frequently Asked

Quick answers

Does sweat actually smell bad?

Fresh sweat is nearly odorless — eccrine sweat is mostly water and salt. Apocrine sweat from armpits contains proteins that bacteria feed on, but the smell itself is bacterial waste, not the sweat.

What bacteria causes body odor?

Staphylococcus hominis is the primary culprit. It converts an odorless apocrine compound into thioalcohols — sulfur molecules your nose detects as armpit smell. Corynebacterium species are also significant contributors.

Why do some people sweat more but smell less?

Sweat volume has little to do with smell intensity. What matters is the bacterial composition of your skin microbiome — some people host populations that produce fewer odorous byproducts regardless of how much they sweat.

Why does my shirt smell even when I don't feel sweaty?

Bacteria embedded in fabric from previous wears remain viable and active. When you wear the shirt again and begin sweating slightly, those bacteria reactivate and quickly produce odor — even if you don't feel like you're sweating.
