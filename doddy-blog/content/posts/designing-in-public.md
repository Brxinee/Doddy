The Smelloff visual identity started with a constraint I didn't choose: I was doing it alone.

No brand studio. No agency deck with three options. Just me, Figma, a browser full of reference images, and the uncomfortable knowledge that whatever I decided would end up on physical packaging that would cost real money to change.

That constraint turned out to be useful. Most bad design decisions happen in meetings. When there's no meeting, there's no consensus-seeking, no "let's try a lighter version of the blue," no death by iteration. You make a call and you move.

Here's how I made mine.

## Why Matte Black and Acid Green

The color decision is the one people ask about most. Matte black with acid green — it reads as aggressive, tech-adjacent, a little edge. People assume it's a vibe choice. It wasn't, or not only.

The actual reasoning was pharmacy shelves.

Personal care in India defaults to white, silver, and light pastel packaging. The shelf is a wall of softness — cream tones, blue gradients, white bottles with lifestyle photography. That's what every established brand gravitates toward because it reads as "clean" and "clinical" and "safe."

Matte black is almost absent from that shelf. Which means a matte black bottle photographs completely differently from everything around it. In a flatlay — which is where most D2C personal care gets sold in the first place — a dark product with an acid green detail pops against a white background in a way that nothing needs an art director to orchestrate.

There was also a readability decision in the green specifically. Acid green (#B8FF57, roughly) reads clearly on black. It also reads clearly on white. So whether the product is photographed against a dark background or a light one, the brand stays legible. You can't say that about a warm yellow or a coral or most of the "statement" colors that look beautiful in isolation and disappear in context.

The green is also not a calm color. It's slightly electric. That matches what the product does — odor neutralization is a functional, almost clinical thing. The green says precision, not wellness. I didn't want Smelloff looking like a spa product.

## Barlow Condensed and the Label Voice

Typography on a small label is a different problem than typography on a screen. You have maybe 40mm of width at most. The name has to be readable at arm's length. And it has to look intentional, not like a default.

I tested a lot of options. Extended sans-serifs looked too corporate. Rounded fonts looked too casual. Serif options either looked traditional-apothecary (unintentional vintage positioning) or trendy-editorial (too self-consciously stylish).

Barlow Condensed worked because it's mechanically neutral. It doesn't have a strong personality of its own — it's a workhorse. Heavy weight Barlow Condensed at a large size reads as confident. All-caps Barlow reads like a product category label on a good factory floor, not a brand with opinions about itself.

That's the voice I wanted on the label. "SMELLOFF. FABRIC ODOR REMOVER." Not styled. Not conceptual. Just accurate and confident.

The contrast in the body copy and interface — I use DM Sans — was intentional. DM Sans is warmer. It rounds at the edges in a way that makes running text feel friendly rather than austere. When there are instructions on the back of the bottle, or when there's copy on the website, the switch from Barlow to DM Sans creates a register shift. BARLOW is the brand stating itself. DM Sans is the brand talking to you.

## The Editorial Influence

The visual reference I kept returning to was not the personal care category at all. It was editorial — specifically the kind of minimal editorial design that byxp.co.uk does well. Considered whitespace. Type doing the work. Photography that isn't over-styled.

Editorial design has a discipline that product design often lacks: nothing gets added unless it earns its place. A layout element that's decorative rather than structural gets removed. The grid matters. The relationship between headline and image matters. Cleverness is expensive — it costs attention, and attention is the only budget you actually have.

Bringing that discipline into a product context means making similar cuts. No decorative borders on the label. No icon set for "features." If something needs an icon, the product probably needs better copy. Icons are a crutch.

The restraint shows most clearly in what's not there.

## The Principle of One More Thing Removed

There's a version of design thinking that asks: what can we add? Feature list, visual detail, option set. The version I find more useful asks: what can we remove without the thing falling apart?

The label went through several rounds of this. An early version had a small icon indicating "safe for all fabrics." I removed it. The copy said it. The icon added visual weight with no additional information. Another version had a product number — SMELLOFF 01 — because numbered product lines look like a considered family, even with one product. I removed it. Numbering something that doesn't have a sequel is self-mythologizing in a way that felt like overreach.

The final label has: the name, the product descriptor, the size, and the usage instruction. That's it. Everything else was subtracted.

This is actually the hardest thing to do, and I think it's more a practiced discipline than a natural instinct. The default instinct is to add. Adding feels productive. Removing feels like giving up something you worked on.

But restraint is the thing that makes a brand feel considered rather than assembled. When you see a label and there's nothing extra on it, you assume the maker knew what they were doing. That assumption is the brand.
