Most deodorant ads show someone sweating in a gym and then confidently walking into a party. The product is always applied to skin. Usually armpits. The promise is: apply this, stop smelling bad, be confident.

I watched those ads for years without questioning them. Then I started noticing something that didn't fit the story.

## The Insight That Actually Started This

People I know — smart, clean people who shower daily — were still anxious about smell. Not because of body odor. Because of their clothes.

A jacket worn twice. A shirt that spent a day folded in a bag. Jeans that absorbed a long commute. Gym clothes that dried but retained something. These are real scenarios. And they're scenarios where a body deodorant does absolutely nothing.

The fear of smelling bad isn't really about sweat. It's about being caught. There's a specific social dread in realizing, mid-conversation, that your shirt carries a smell from three days ago. That moment is worse than anything the ads are selling against.

Deodorants solve the wrong problem for a lot of real smell anxiety. They address the skin. The clothes go untouched.

That was the gap. That became Smelloff.

## Why Fabric-Only Was the Right Bet

The obvious question is: why not just make another deo? The market's huge, the CAC is understood, the category works.

The answer is that the market being huge also means the market is brutal. You'd need massive ad budgets to compete against Fogg and Engage and every other brand that has been building distribution for years. And you'd be making something that, functionally, is already made well by many brands.

Fabric mist as a category barely existed in India. A few premium international brands sold something adjacent at mall perfume counters. Nothing was positioned for the practical, everyday anxiety I was describing. Nothing said: this is specifically for clothes, specifically for the smell that survives the wash, specifically for the moment before you walk out the door.

A narrow product in an empty space beats a generic product in a crowded one. That's a bet you can actually win.

There's also a usage behavior that works in our favor. People already mist their clothes with water to remove wrinkles. They already spray perfume on fabric. The behavior of applying something to clothes before wearing isn't weird — it just needed a dedicated product with a clear reason to exist.

Smelloff is that product. Fabric mist, not deo. The label says what it does.

## Building Solo in Hyderabad

I didn't start with a team. I still don't have one, in the traditional sense.

Hyderabad is a good city to build something from scratch. The cost structure is forgiving. You can rent a workspace, get a lab to run formulations, find a contract manufacturer, and stay lean in a way that's much harder in Mumbai or Bangalore. There's a certain amount of unsexy practical advantage to being here.

Going solo has a real cost though: you make every decision alone, and there's no one to tell you when you're wrong until the market does it. That's uncomfortable in a specific way that you can't fully prepare for. An instinct that feels solid for two weeks can fall apart the moment you test it on a hundred orders.

What I found was that solo building forces a kind of honesty with yourself that teams can accidentally avoid. There's no internal debate where someone else gets overruled. You own every choice. Which means you also have to be honest about which choices were good and which were you being stubborn.

The design work, the copy, the formulation brief, the packaging decisions, the pricing math — I did all of that myself. Not because I think I'm uniquely skilled in every area, but because at the stage Smelloff was at, bringing in specialists before the fundamentals were clear would have been expensive in the wrong direction.

## The ₹159 Price Point

Pricing a new category is genuinely hard. There's no reference price in the customer's head because there's no category.

I could have gone premium — ₹400-500, nice bottle, feel like a luxury item. I could have gone budget — ₹99, volume play, figure out margins later. Both had arguments.

I went with ₹159 because it's the price of a considered, slightly elevated everyday purchase. It's close enough to what someone would spend on a mid-range room freshener or a small personal care item that it doesn't require justification. But it's above the range where the product seems disposable or low-effort.

It also had to work with the unit economics. At ₹159, with a 100ml bottle, the contribution margin is viable without needing massive volume out of the gate. I could run a genuine business at that price rather than needing hockey-stick growth to make the numbers work.

The price is the promise as much as the product is. ₹159 says: this is a real product, not an experiment. You're not paying a premium-brand tax. You're paying a fair price for something that works.

## What ODORSTRIKE Actually Is

I want to be clear about what the product does, because some of the claims in this space are vague and I don't want to be vague.

ODORSTRIKE is the active system in Smelloff. It's not a masking agent. A masking agent puts fragrance on top of an odor. You're still smelling the original thing — it's just buried under something more pleasant. That approach works for about 40 minutes.

ODORSTRIKE works through odor neutralization at a molecular level. The odor compounds that fabric holds — the ones that survive washing, the ones from sweat and cooking and urban commutes — have specific chemical structures. The active ingredient binds to those structures and breaks the chain before they volatilize into the air. Less volatilization, less odor reaching anyone's nose.

This is why the product can be unscented or very lightly scented. You don't need to dump perfume on it to cover the odor because you're not covering — you're neutralizing.

I worked with a formulation lab to get this right. The first versions were over-fragranced because the instinct in this category is always to make something smell good. I pushed to take the fragrance down because I wanted people to trust the function, not the smell.

If you spray Smelloff on a garment and then can't smell anything, that's the product working. That's not a failure of the product — that's the point.
